Overview

Illegal transactions have been a central part of bitcoin story since the rise and fall of Silk Road. Albeit its dark side, bitcoinâ€™s usage has kept on increasing. Law enforcement and anti-money-laundering regulation therefore becomes important to prevent it from being used by criminals and terrorists.

There are numerous attempts to identify illicit transactions in bitcoin, yet most of them only considered local, static features instead of its graph structure. One may imagine that illicit transactions tend to occur in neighborhoods, hence abandoning the graph structure of bitcoin is a waste of information that can potentially improve prediction accuracy.

For this dissertation, we would like to explore the use of graph representation learning to identify illicit transactions in the bitcoin transaction graph.

Literature Review

There are numerous publications on bitcoin address classification already, for example [1], [2], [3], [4]. Yet, they didn't focus on bitcoin's inherent graph structure.

[5] has considered bitcoin as a hypergraph and suggested a motif structure to identify exchange. Yet it is still not fully utilizing the graph topology in full.

[6] is the most similar study to our goal - it has considered the bitcoin's graph structure and used graph convolution network [7] as node embeddings for identify illict bitcoin transactions. Furthermore, it has also provided a dataset, called the Elliptic Dataset, for public research and development on bitcoin AML algorithm.

Proposed methodology

Our work will be based on [6], and extend it by testing more graph learning techniques, including:

  • DeepWalk [8]
  • node2vec [9]
  • GraphSAGE [10]
We would try to further improve the GraphSAGE algorithm to fit the Elliptic dataset to enhance the prediction accuracy.
